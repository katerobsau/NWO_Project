Compound Events

Individually, both extreme rainfall and storm surge have the potential to produce flooding. However,compared with either event occurring individual, the potential risk is magnified if these events co-occur. This is what is known as a compound event.

Definition 1: The IPCC SREX (Ref 7, p. 118) defines a compound event as:
(1) two or more extreme events occurring simultaneously or successively,
(2) combinations of extreme events with underlying conditions that amplify the impact of the events, or
(3) combinations of events that are not themselves extremes but lead
to an extreme event or impact when combined.
The contributing events can be of similar (clustered multiple events) or different type(s).

Rainfall-Surge Mechansims

Wahl et al. (2015) breaks the compound event involving rainfall-surge down into three mechanism types.

Mechanism (1) in estuarine regions, the joint occurrence of both may elevate water levels to a point where flooding is initiated or its impacts exacerbated.
Mechanism (2) occurs when a destructive storm surge already causes widespread flooding, such that any significant rainfall on top of this—even if it is not an extreme event on its own—increases the flood depth and/or extent of the inundated area.
Mechanism (3) occurs during a moderate storm surge which does not directly cause flooding, but is high enough to fully block or slow down gravity-fed storm water drainage, such that precipitation is more likely to cause flooding.

An example of the risk regions related to these three mechanisms is shown in Figure NULL 1.

Mechanisms in the Netherlands

In the Netherlands, flood risk in the canal belt system is heavily mitigated by gravity-based sluices (Figure NULL 2). These sluices are only operational when the internal water level is higher than the external level and the water can be discharged using gravity. For many sluices in the Netherlands, the difference between water levels needed for operation is 0.1 m, any less than this and water can flow in the opposite direction due to density differences between fresh and salt water.

Schematic of the gravity based sluice at Harlingen. Water can be discharged from the sluice provided there is a water level on the canal belt side is 0.1 m higher or more than the sea water level. The normal height of water in the canal belt system and optimal for operational purposes is -0.52 m (NAP) (Green dashed line). Operators are concerned for flooding risk when the inland water level is in excess of -0.32 m (NAP) (red dotted line).

Rethinking the Risk Framework

An important distinction when assessing the risk of rainfall-surge events is that in the Netherlands the height of the storm-surge does not necessarily need to be extreme. Instead, of concern is

  • Does the total sea level (tide + surge) exceed the risk threshold?

  • How long does the total level stay above this risk thresehold, preventing discharge from the sluice?

In this sense the statistical problem becomes a censored one as we are not concerned with the extemeness of the surge height. Once the surge contributes to the total level exceeding the risk threshold, it doesn’t matter by how much the threshold is exceeded. Either way, water can not be discharged. This requires a fundamental reframing of the statistical problem, and move away from the traditional statistical approach of assessing risk by modelling the dependence between surge heights and rainfall amounts.

The reframing of the problem may also be advantageous. In misspecifying the problem, risk is not reliably assessed. Additionally, the dependence between extreme rainfall and extreme surge heights is asymptotically weak at many locations throughout the world, including the Netherlands. (cite wahl, westra, bevacqua). This weak dependence is difficult to accurately estimate (westra). A rephrasing of the problem may improve the reliability of the estimates for the dependence parameters.

Event Definition

In order to estimate the probability of the compound-event occuring, a set of variables needs to be defined to model the problem. These variables are given in Table NULL 4. We have adopted a similar problem specification to Li et al. 2014, who considered an application of wave heights for Dutch storms. Namely in our specification, we are interested in the duration that the gravity based is not operational.

Variable Name Variable Type
T_s Duration of the storm surge
M_s Maximum surge height during T_s
h_t Threshold water level that water needs to be discharged to prevent flood risk
T_d Duration that the gravity based sluice is not operational
T_p Minimum period of time the sluice needs to be operational to relieve flood risk
